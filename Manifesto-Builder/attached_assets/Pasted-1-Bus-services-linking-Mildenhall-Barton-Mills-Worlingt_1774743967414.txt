2. Bus passes: what we can say (England & Suffolk level)
Scheme basics
Who gets a pass?

Older person pass: from State Pension age (currently 66, rising over time). 

Disabled person pass: if you meet defined disability criteria (blind/partially sighted, profoundly deaf, substantial mobility issues, etc.). 

Administered locally by Suffolk County Council under the English National Concessionary Travel Scheme (ENCTS). 

England‑wide picture
Latest DfT concessionary travel stats for England show: 

8.9 million older and disabled concessionary passes in England.

Roughly 90% are “older” passes, 10% disabled.

Take‑up of older people’s passes is typically around 70–80% of eligible older residents, higher in urban conurbations, slightly lower in rural areas. 

So, at England level you can truthfully say:

“Around three‑quarters of people old enough to qualify for a free bus pass actually hold one, and about one in ten passes nationally are issued on disability grounds rather than age.”

Suffolk level
Suffolk is a non‑metropolitan Travel Concession Authority within ENCTS; its passes and journeys are included in the national stats, but published tables are at county/area type level, not parish. 

Suffolk County Council runs the scheme and the My Bus Pass portal, but public‑facing pages do not publish numbers of passes by parish or division. 

3. What you’re asking that doesn’t exist publicly (yet)
You asked for:

“Population that use them” (i.e. concessionary journeys) for Mildenhall, Barton Mills, Worlington, Freckenham.

“Bus passes issued in [each parish]”, split into retired vs disabled.

“What percentage of the population in these areas have a bus pass”, compared to Suffolk and England.

Based on the official data structure:

DfT concessionary stats are collected at Travel Concession Authority level (so: Suffolk as a whole), not by parish or ward. 

Suffolk’s own public pages don’t break down pass counts or usage by parish. 

So there is no published dataset that will directly tell you:

“X% of residents in Worlington hold an older person’s bus pass and Y% hold a disabled pass.”

To get that, you’d need internal SCC data.

If you want to go beyond “national picture” and make this local and sharp, the realistic route is:

Ask SCC (Concessionary Travel / Passenger Transport) for:

ONS population denominators

Compare to England/Suffolk averages

~8.9m passes nationally, ~90% older / 10% disabled.

That would let you say things like:

“In Worlington and Freckenham, X% of residents hold a concessionary bus pass, compared with Y% across Suffolk and Z% across England, yet they rely on a single infrequent bus route (357).”
